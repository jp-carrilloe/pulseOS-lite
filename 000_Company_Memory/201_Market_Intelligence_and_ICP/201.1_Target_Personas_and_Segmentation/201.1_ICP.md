# Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) & Persona Definitions — PulseOS

## Purpose
Define the current source of truth for PulseOS and Tintto’s immediate commercial ICP, so that research, targeting, outreach, offer design, and qualification all point to the same customer profile.

This version reflects the current Segment A decision:

- scale-up operators first
- Europe first, US second
- structural execution pain first
- AI adoption failure through fragmentation as the main wedge

## Strategic ICP Frame

The current ICP is not defined primarily by industry.

It is defined by **structural conditions**:

- operational complexity is rising
- execution is getting slower or less visible
- multiple tools are in use
- leadership wants to adopt AI
- AI implementation is failing because the company context is fragmented

The most important insight is:

> We are not targeting companies that want AI.  
> We are targeting companies that can no longer operate efficiently and are looking for a structural fix.

## Executive Summary

- **Ideal customer:** €10M to €150M revenue companies with 20 to 200 employees and visible operational complexity
- **Sweet spot:** 40 to 120 employees and €15M to €80M revenue
- **Best segment:** B2B SaaS first, then agencies / consulting firms, then tech-enabled services and selected PE portfolio companies
- **Primary buyers:** Founder / CEO, COO, Head of Operations
- **Secondary buyers:** Head of RevOps / Ops
- **Primary pain:** execution friction caused by fragmentation, unclear ownership, and systems that do not work together
- **Winning diagnosis:** the company structure is the bottleneck, not the tools or the people

## Primary ICP — Segment A: Scale-Up Operators

> A scaling company where execution is breaking under complexity, and leadership knows it, but does not yet have a structural solution.

### Company Profile

- **Employees:** 20 to 200
- **Sweet spot:** 40 to 120
- **Revenue:** €10M to €150M
- **Ideal revenue band:** €15M to €80M
- **Stage:** post-product-market fit and actively scaling
- **Geography:** Europe first, US second

### Why this segment works

- enough budget for €50K to €100K
- pain is real and daily
- decision-makers are accessible
- not blocked by enterprise procurement

## Industry Prioritization

### Tier 1

#### B2B SaaS

- multiple tools
- fast scaling
- strong AI interest
- high information density

#### Agencies / Consulting firms

- project chaos
- client data fragmentation
- immediate ROI from process clarity

### Tier 2

#### Private equity portfolio companies

- optimization pressure
- inefficiencies are costly and visible

#### Tech-enabled services

- hybrid workflows
- fragmented systems
- operational handoffs create friction

### Avoid early

- heavily regulated industries
- hardware and manufacturing
- very small startups
- low-complexity businesses

## Buyer Personas

### Primary Buyer — Founder / CEO

#### What they feel

- lack of visibility
- slow execution
- tool overload
- too much dependence on manual coordination

#### Why they buy

- they feel chaos in the company margin
- they see execution lag directly
- they want a structural solution, not another tool layer

### Primary Buyer — COO / Head of Operations

#### What they feel

- process breakdown
- coordination overhead
- reporting pain
- unclear ownership across functions

#### Why they buy

- they own the operational pain every day
- they understand the cost of fragmentation directly
- they are often the fastest path to a real implementation mandate

### Secondary Buyer — Head of RevOps / Ops

#### What they feel

- system fragmentation
- duplicated data
- broken handoffs
- manual reporting and process workarounds

#### Why they matter

- they understand the systems deeply
- they can champion the project internally
- they often supply the strongest pain evidence

### Not the initial buyer

- CTO
- Head of AI
- IT

These roles may validate later, but they are usually too technical, too experimental, or too risk-framed for the initial sales motion.

## Pain Signals

### Strong Signals

- hiring for:
  - RevOps
  - AI Ops
  - Data roles
  - Automation roles

- explicit language such as:
  - “we want to use AI internally”
  - “we need better visibility”
  - “our tools do not talk to each other”
  - “we have too much manual reporting”

### Medium Signals

- visible use of:
  - HubSpot or Salesforce
  - Notion
  - Slack
  - multiple project tools

- visible symptoms:
  - manual reporting
  - duplicated data
  - unclear ownership
  - cross-functional workflow friction

### Red Flags

- “everything works fine”
- no urgency
- no operational complexity
- purely exploratory AI interest without real execution pain

## Economic Profile

### Budget Logic

€50K to €100K is viable if:

- the company spends €1M+ annually on payroll
- or spends €100K+ annually on tools and operations

### Rule of Thumb

If saving 1 to 2 FTEs or increasing output by 20% is realistic, the engagement is economically easy to justify.

### ROI Framing

The offer should be framed around:

- faster execution
- fewer coordination costs
- better decisions

## Urgency Triggers

### Best Timing Moments

- post-funding
- scaling team
- hiring ops roles
- tool migration chaos
- failed AI experiments

### Worst Timing Moments

- stable or slow-growth companies
- pre-product-market fit
- cost-cutting survival mode

## Deal Characteristics

### Ideal Deal

- 2 to 3 calls
- decision-maker involved early
- clear pain acknowledged

### Bad Deal

- long stakeholder chains
- “just exploring AI”
- unclear ownership

## Psychological Profile

The ideal buyer already believes:

- something is broken
- tools are not enough
- execution is slower than it should be

But they often cannot articulate:

- why
- where
- how to fix it

The winning line is:

> Your company is not structured for AI execution.

## Fast ICP Checklist

Before engaging, check:

- 20 to 200 employees
- €10M+ revenue
- multiple tools in use
- hiring ops, data, or automation roles
- signs of growth or complexity
- founder or COO reachable

If yes, the company is a strong candidate.

## Exclusion Criteria

- very small startups with no real process layer yet
- traditional enterprises with heavy procurement drag
- companies with no visible operational complexity
- companies in stable mode with no timing trigger
- companies seeking AI experimentation without execution accountability

## Recommendation in One Line

The best current ICP is:

> A scaling company where execution is already expensive because complexity is outrunning structure.
